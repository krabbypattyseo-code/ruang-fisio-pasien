# Therapeutic Exercise — Foundations and Techniques

Source: *Therapeutic Exercise: Foundations and Techniques* by Kisner & Colby (5th ed., F.A. Davis, 2007)

---

## Part I: Foundational Concepts

### Disablement Model (Nagi / ICF Framework)

| Level | Description | PT Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Pathology** | Disease, injury, or disorder at cellular/tissue level | Medical diagnosis; background knowledge |
| **Impairment** | Signs/symptoms at body system, organ, or tissue level | Identify and measure (ROM, strength, pain, balance) |
| **Functional limitation** | Reduced ability to perform component motor tasks | Task analysis; ADL/IADL performance |
| **Disability** | Inability to perform life roles (self-care, work, recreation, community) | Patient-centered goals; quality of life |

**ICF Terms:** Impairment of body structure/function → Activity limitation → Participation restriction

**Key principle:** Reducing impairments does not automatically restore function. Integrate task-specific training alongside impairment-level interventions.

### Patient Management Model (5-Step Cycle)

1. **Examination** — Health history + systems review + specific tests/measures
2. **Evaluation** — Interpret data; identify impairments, functional limitations, disability
3. **Diagnosis** — PT diagnosis based on movement dysfunction
4. **Prognosis + Plan of Care** — Goals (short/long-term), interventions, expected outcomes
5. **Intervention** — Execute + re-examine; adjust as needed

**Types of impairments managed with therapeutic exercise:**
- Musculoskeletal: pain, limited ROM, weakness, faulty posture, muscle imbalance
- Neuromuscular: impaired balance, incoordination, abnormal tone, delayed motor development
- Cardiovascular/pulmonary: decreased aerobic capacity, impaired circulation
- Integumentary: skin hypomobility, adherent scarring

### Motor Learning — Stages and Practice

**Three Stages of Motor Learning:**

| Stage | Characteristics | Therapist Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive** | Figuring out what/how to do; high errors; needs attention | Frequent feedback; simple environment; demonstration |
| **Associative** | Fine-tuning; fewer errors; exploring variations | Reduce feedback frequency; introduce variability |
| **Autonomous** | Automatic; dual-tasking; adapts to environment | Minimal input; complex open environments |

**Practice Types:**
- **Blocked** → fastest early performance gains (same task, same conditions)
- **Random** → best retention and generalizability (varying tasks in unpredictable order)
- **Random-blocked** → balance between acquisition speed and retention
- **Part vs. Whole** → part practice for complex serial tasks; whole practice for continuous/discrete tasks
- **Mental practice** + physical practice → faster acquisition than physical alone

**Feedback:**
- **Knowledge of Performance (KP):** quality of movement during/after task
- **Knowledge of Results (KR):** outcome of task (was the goal achieved?)
- Reduce augmented feedback frequency over time to promote self-monitoring
- Focus patient attention on task outcomes (external focus) > movement details (internal focus)

## Part II: Applied Science of Exercise

### Chapter 3: Range of Motion (ROM)

**Types:**
- **PROM** — therapist-controlled; no active muscle contraction; used when active motion is contraindicated
- **AROM** — patient-generated; maintains strength and neuromuscular coordination
- **AROM-assistive** — patient initiates; therapist or device assists through range

**Indications:** Maintain joint/connective tissue mobility; promote synovial fluid movement; prevent contracture; maintain awareness of movement.

**Contraindications:** Disrupts healing process; PROM contraindicated when active motion is indicated; ROM contraindicated if motion is disruptive to healing.

**Continuous Passive Motion (CPM):** Post-surgical cartilage/tendon repair; promotes synovial fluid diffusion, prevents adhesions; use 8–24 hrs/day; set limits based on tissue tolerance.

### Chapter 4: Stretching for Impaired Mobility

**Key Definitions:**
- **Flexibility:** ROM available at a joint
- **Hypomobility:** decreased mobility due to soft tissue restriction
- **Contracture:** persistent adaptive shortening of soft tissue
  - *Myostatic*: shortened without structural changes
  - *Pseudomyostatic*: hypertonicity-driven
  - *Arthrogenic/periarticular*: capsule/ligament restriction
  - *Fibrotic/irreversible*: structural tissue changes

**Mechanical Properties of Soft Tissue:**
- **Elastic deformation** — reversible; returns to original length after force removal
- **Plastic deformation** — permanent change in length after sustained force
- **Creep** — progressive elongation under constant low load over time (target for stretching)
- **Stress relaxation** — decreased force needed to maintain length over time

**Neurophysiological Basis:**
- **Muscle spindle (Ia afferents):** detects stretch → reflex contraction (stretch reflex) — minimize by slow stretch
- **Golgi Tendon Organ (GTO):** detects tension → autogenic inhibition → muscle relaxation — activated by contraction preceding stretch (PNF CR method)
- **Reciprocal inhibition:** contracting antagonist relaxes target muscle

**Stretching Parameters:**

| Parameter | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Intensity** | Low-load preferred; point of mild discomfort, not pain |
| **Duration** | 15–30 sec static; 30–60 sec for older adults; longer (1–2 min) for fibrotic tissue |
| **Frequency** | ≥5 days/week for early gains; 3×/week for maintenance |
| **Speed** | Slow and sustained; avoid ballistic for inflamed/fragile tissue |
| **Mode** | Static, cyclic, PNF; heat + stretch synergistic |

**PNF Stretching Techniques:**
- **Hold-Relax (HR):** PROM → isometric contraction of target muscle (6–10 sec) → relax → passive stretch further
- **Contract-Relax (CR):** target muscle contracts isotonically through range → then passively stretched
- **Contract-Relax Agonist Contract (CRAC):** CR + active contraction of antagonist during final stretch (autogenic + reciprocal inhibition)

**Precautions:** Never stretch through pain; avoid aggressive stretching of acutely inflamed tissue, bony blocks, post-fracture, or hypermobile joints.

**Adjuncts:** Heat (increases tissue extensibility), massage, joint traction/oscillation, biofeedback, relaxation training.

### Chapter 5: Peripheral Joint Mobilization

**Arthrokinematics — Convex-Concave Rule:**
- **Convex on concave** (e.g., femoral head in acetabulum): joint surface rolls and glides in **opposite** directions
- **Concave on convex** (e.g., tibial plateau on femur): roll and glide in **same** direction
- Grades assess treatment direction based on restriction pattern

**Grades of Mobilization (Maitland):**
| Grade | Description | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|
| I | Small-amplitude oscillations at beginning of range | Pain management |
| II | Large-amplitude oscillations, not reaching end range | Pain management |
| III | Large-amplitude oscillations at end of range | Stretch hypomobile structures |
| IV | Small-amplitude oscillations at end of range | Stretch hypomobile structures |
| V | Thrust (manipulation) | Specific indications only |

**Indications:** Pain + muscle guarding; reversible joint hypomobility; positional faults/subluxations; progressive limitation; functional immobility.

**Contraindications:** Hypermobility, joint effusion, active inflammation, fracture, osteoporosis, malignancy, infection.

**Mobilization with Movement (MWM — Mulligan):** Therapist applies a sustained accessory glide while patient performs active movement; should be pain-free; used for positional faults.

### Chapter 6: Resistance Exercise

**Key Definitions:**
- **Strength:** Maximum force a muscle can generate (1RM)
- **Power:** Force × velocity (rate of force production)
- **Endurance:** Ability to sustain sub-maximal effort

**Principles:**
- **Overload:** Stimulus must exceed current capacity to elicit adaptation
- **SAID:** Specific Adaptation to Imposed Demands — train how you want to perform
- **Reversibility:** Gains lost within weeks if training stops (detraining)
- **Periodization:** Systematic variation of volume/intensity to prevent plateau and overtraining

**Determinants of Resistance Exercise:**
| Variable | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Intensity | 60–80% 1RM for strength; 40–60% 1RM for endurance |
| Volume | 1–3 sets × 8–12 reps (strength); 15–25+ reps (endurance) |
| Frequency | 2–3×/week; allow 48-hr recovery per muscle group |
| Rest interval | 2–3 min (strength); 30–90 sec (endurance) |
| Velocity | Slow–moderate for beginners; faster/plyometric for power |
| Order | Large before small muscle groups; multi-joint before single-joint |

**Types of Muscle Contraction:**
- **Isometric (static):** No joint movement; useful when movement is painful or contraindicated; 6-sec holds at multiple angles
- **Concentric:** Muscle shortens; primary type in most strengthening exercises
- **Eccentric:** Muscle lengthens under load; higher force production; key for tendinopathy rehab and deceleration training; delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) risk
- **Isokinetic:** Constant velocity; requires specialized equipment; useful for assessment and sport rehab

**Open-Chain (OKC) vs. Closed-Chain (CKC):**
| Feature | Open Chain | Closed Chain |
|---|---|---|
| Distal segment | Free (not fixed) | Fixed (weight-bearing) |
| Primary use | Isolated muscle strengthening | Functional, co-contraction, proprioception |
| Joint stress | Higher shear (e.g., ACL anterior tibial shear) | Compressive; more joint stability |
| Examples | Leg extension, bicep curl | Squat, lunge, push-up, step-up |

**PNF Resistance Patterns:**
- **UE D1 Flexion:** Hand to opposite shoulder (shoulder flexion/adduction/ER + forearm supination)
- **UE D1 Extension:** Shoulder extension/abduction/IR + forearm pronation
- **UE D2 Flexion:** Hand to top of head (shoulder flexion/abduction/ER)
- **UE D2 Extension:** Shoulder extension/adduction/IR
- **LE D1 Flexion:** Hip flexion/adduction/ER + ankle DF/inversion
- **LE D2 Flexion:** Hip flexion/abduction/IR + ankle DF/eversion

**PNF Specific Techniques:**
- **Rhythmic initiation:** Passive → active-assistive → active; for hypertonia, rigidity
- **Hold-relax (agonist contraction):** Relax spastic or antagonist tone; improve ROM
- **Repeated contractions:** Increase agonist strength through repeated maximal effort
- **Slow reversal / slow reversal hold:** Alternating agonist/antagonist activation; improve strength and coordination
- **Rhythmic stabilization:** Alternating isometric contractions; improve stability

**Selected Training Regimens:**
- **DeLorme Progressive Resistance Exercise (PRE):** 3 × 10 at 50%, 75%, 100% of 10RM; increase load weekly
- **Circuit Training:** Low rest; combines strength + aerobic benefit
- **Plyometrics (stretch-shortening drills):** Eccentric → concentric (amortization phase minimized); for power and sport-specific training; requires adequate base strength first

**Precautions:** Valsalva maneuver (avoid breath-holding); substitute motions; overtraining; DOMS; pathological fracture risk (osteoporosis, bone metastases).

### Chapter 7: Aerobic Exercise

**FITT Principles:**
| Component | Guideline |
|---|---|
| **Frequency** | 3–5 days/week |
| **Intensity** | 50–85% VO₂max; 60–90% HRmax; RPE 12–16 (Borg scale) |
| **Duration** | 20–60 min continuous aerobic; or multiple 10-min bouts |
| **Mode** | Walking, cycling, swimming, elliptical; cross-training preferred |

**Intensity Estimation:**
- **HRmax = 220 – age** (approximate)
- **Target HR = HRmax × 0.6–0.9** (60–90% range)
- **Karvonen Formula:** Target HR = [(HRmax – HRrest) × %intensity] + HRrest

**Phases of a Session:** Warm-up (5–10 min) → Aerobic phase → Cool-down (5–10 min)

**Training Adaptations:** ↑ stroke volume and cardiac output; ↓ resting HR; ↑ mitochondrial density; ↑ VO₂max; ↑ fat oxidation.

**Cardiac Rehab Phases:**
- Phase I (inpatient): METs 1–3; monitored; 3–5 min sessions
- Phase II (outpatient): Guided; progressive intensity
- Phase III (community): Maintenance; independent

### Chapter 8: Balance Training

**Balance Control Systems:**
- **Somatosensory** (most heavily used on stable surfaces)
- **Visual** (backup; dominant when somatosensory is unreliable)
- **Vestibular** (dominant on unstable surfaces or with visual/somatosensory conflict)

**Motor Strategies for Balance:**
| Strategy | When Used |
|---|---|
| **Ankle strategy** | Small, slow perturbations on firm surface |
| **Hip strategy** | Larger/faster perturbations; narrow surfaces |
| **Stepping strategy** | When COM exceeds BOS |
| **Suspensory strategy** | Reduces COM height; low-clearance activities |

**Balance Training Progression:**
1. Stable surface → unstable surface
2. Wide BOS → narrow BOS → single leg → tandem
3. Eyes open → eyes closed → head turns
4. Static → dynamic tasks
5. Predictable → unpredictable perturbations
6. Single task → dual task (cognitive + motor)

**Evidence-Based Programs:** Otago Exercise Programme; Tai Chi (fall prevention in older adults); FallProof™; vestibular rehabilitation.

### Chapter 9: Aquatic Exercise

**Properties of Water for Therapy:**
- **Buoyancy:** Reduces effective body weight (≈50% at waist depth, ≈75% at chest depth); reduces joint load
- **Hydrostatic pressure:** Reduces edema; assists venous return; increases respiratory load
- **Resistance:** Water provides resistance proportional to speed and frontal surface area
- **Temperature:** Warm water (33–36°C) reduces muscle tone, decreases pain, increases extensibility

**Indications:** Non-weight-bearing early rehab; aquatic conditioning; balance and gait training; arthritis; post-surgical.

**Contraindications:** Open wounds, urinary/bowel incontinence, infectious skin conditions, severe cardiopulmonary instability.

## Part III: Principles of Intervention

### Chapter 10: Soft Tissue Injury Management

**Stages of Healing and Management:**

| Stage | Timeframe | Tissue Response | Management Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute (Inflammation)** | Days 1–6 | Inflammation, edema, pain | PRICE; protect; control pain/swelling; PROM if tolerated |
| **Subacute (Repair/Proliferation)** | Day 4 – Week 3 | Collagen synthesis; fragile tissue | Controlled motion; AROM; early isometrics; scar management |
| **Chronic (Remodeling/Maturation)** | Week 3 – months | Collagen cross-linking; tissue strengthening | Progressive loading; strength; function; return to activity |

**PRICE (Acute):** Protection, Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation

**Cumulative Trauma / Chronic Recurring Pain:**
- Caused by repetitive microtrauma, poor ergonomics, prolonged immobility, or inadequate recovery
- Manage: Activity modification; ergonomic correction; progressive loading; flexibility + strength; address contributing factors (posture, technique)

### Chapter 11: Joint, Connective Tissue, and Bone Disorders

**Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA):**
- Inflammatory; systemic; joint destruction; morning stiffness >1 hour
- Exercise: Low-to-moderate intensity AROM + gentle strengthening in subacute/remission; avoid exercise during acute flares; protect joints
- Precautions: Cervical spine instability (atlantoaxial); osteoporosis risk; fatigue management

**Osteoarthritis (OA):**
- Degenerative; mechanical; joint space narrowing; crepitus; stiffness after rest
- Exercise: Aquatic or land-based aerobic + strengthening effective; ROM maintenance; unloading strategies; proprioceptive training
- Evidence: Quadriceps strengthening reduces knee OA symptoms

**Fibromyalgia:**
- Widespread pain, fatigue, tender points; no inflammation
- Exercise: Low-intensity aerobic (aquatic preferred); graded activity; avoid overexertion; pace

**Myofascial Pain Syndrome:**
- Trigger points; referred pain pattern; limited ROM
- Management: Soft tissue techniques; stretching trigger point muscles; strengthening; address postural factors

**Osteoporosis:**
- Weight-bearing aerobic + resistance exercise for prevention and management
- Precautions: Avoid loaded spinal flexion (vertebral fracture risk); fall prevention priority

**Fracture Rehabilitation:**
- Period of immobilization → adjacent joint ROM + AROM; isometrics within cast
- Post-immobilization: Restore ROM, strength, proprioception; graduated weight-bearing per tissue healing

---

### Chapter 12: Postoperative Management

**Preoperative Management:** Patient education; home exercise instruction; assess baseline function; optimize strength/flexibility.

**Postoperative Principles:**
- Control pain + edema: ice, elevation, compression
- Prevent complications: DVT (early movement, anticoagulation); pulmonary complications (breathing exercises)
- Protect healing tissue: respect tissue constraints, graft healing timelines
- Gradual progressive loading per tissue type (ligament/tendon repair vs. bone vs. cartilage)

**Common Surgical Categories:**
- Repair, reattachment, reconstruction, stabilization of soft tissue
- Release, lengthening, or decompression of soft tissue
- Joint procedures (arthroplasty, arthroscopic debridement)
- Extra-articular bony procedures (osteotomy, ORIF)

---

### Chapter 13: Peripheral Nerve Disorders

**Nerve Injury Classification:**
| Grade | Severity | Recovery |
|---|---|---|
| **Neuropraxia** | Conduction block; no axon disruption | Complete; weeks |
| **Axonotmesis** | Axon disruption; endoneurium intact | Complete but slow; 1mm/day axon regrowth |
| **Neurotmesis** | Complete nerve disruption | Incomplete; may need surgery |

**Neural Tension Disorders:** Adverse neural mechanosensitivity; symptoms reproduced by nerve tension tests
- Upper quadrant: Upper limb neurodynamic tests (ULNT 1–4)
- Lower quadrant: SLR, slump, prone knee bend (femoral nerve)
- Management: Neural mobilization (sliders and tensioners); soft tissue; postural correction

**Common Conditions:**
- **Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS):** Compression of brachial plexus/subclavian vessels; postural correction; scalene/pec minor stretching; scapular stabilization; neural mobilization
- **Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS):** Median nerve at wrist; nerve gliding; wrist ROM; ergonomic modification; wrist splinting (neutral)
- **CRPS:** Desensitization; graded motor imagery; mirror therapy; active movement program

## Part IV: Exercise by Body Region — Key Principles

### Spine and Posture (Ch. 14–16)

**Postural Impairments:**
- **Forward head:** Suboccipital and sternocleidomastoid tight; deep cervical flexors weak
- **Kyphosis:** Thoracic extensors weak; pectoral/anterior chest tight
- **Lordosis:** Hip flexors and lumbar extensors tight; abdominals and gluteals weak
- **Scoliosis:** Side-specific muscle imbalances; 3D deformity management

**Spinal Stabilization Approach:**
1. Activate and coordinate deep stabilizers (transversus abdominis, multifidus, pelvic floor)
2. Progress to global muscle activation with maintained neutral spine
3. Integrate into functional tasks and activity-specific training

**Lumbar Exercise Guidelines:**
- Stabilization (motor control) exercises: evidence for CLBP, disc herniation, spondylolisthesis
- McKenzie extension: disc herniation with centralization of symptoms
- Flexion exercises: stenosis, spondylosis (opens intervertebral foramen)
- Manual therapy + exercise superior to either alone for acute/subacute LBP

---

### Shoulder (Ch. 17)

**Phases of Shoulder Rehab (Post-Surgical or Rotator Cuff Injury):**
1. **Acute:** Immobilization/protection; pendulum; PROM within tissue limits
2. **Subacute:** AROM; rotator cuff activation (ER emphasis); scapular stabilizers
3. **Strengthening:** Progressive resistance; closed-chain; proprioception
4. **Return to function:** Sport-specific; overhead activities; plyometric progression

**Key Targets:** RC (ER/IR); scapular stabilizers (serratus anterior, lower/middle trapezius); posterior capsule flexibility; glenohumeral IR/ER balance.

---

### Elbow (Ch. 18) / Wrist & Hand (Ch. 19)

**Common Conditions:**
- Lateral epicondylalgia: eccentric wrist extensor; progressive loading; avoid pain with exercise
- Medial epicondylalgia: eccentric wrist flexor; technique correction
- CTS: median nerve gliding; wrist/finger tendon gliding; neutral wrist splinting

**Key Principles:** Early controlled motion to prevent adhesion; tendon gliding exercises post-repair; splinting to protect healing structures.

---

### Hip (Ch. 20)

**Key Targets:** Gluteus medius (single-leg stability); hip flexor flexibility; gluteus maximus strength; hip external rotators; core integration.

**Hip Arthroplasty Precautions (Posterior Approach):**
- No hip flexion >90°, no IR, no adduction past midline (6–12 weeks post-op)
- Exercise: Gluteal sets, quad sets, ankle pumps → progressive mobilization → ambulation training

---

### Knee (Ch. 21)

**ACL Rehab — General Milestones:**
| Phase | Goal | Key Exercises |
|---|---|---|
| Early | Control effusion; ROM; quad activation | Quad sets; SLR; PROM; ice/elevation |
| Mid | Full ROM; strength ≥60% | OKC/CKC progression; balance; bike |
| Late | Strength ≥80%; functional | Plyometrics; agility; sport drills |

**Patellofemoral Pain:** VMO strengthening; hip ER/abductor strengthening; patellar taping; avoid deep knee flexion early; step-up/step-down progression.

---

### Ankle and Foot (Ch. 22)

**Ankle Sprain Rehab:**
1. PRICE; protected weight-bearing
2. AROM (alphabet, pumps); proprioception (single-leg stance)
3. Strengthening (resistance band eversion/inversion); calf raises
4. Functional: Heel-toe walking; balance board; sport-specific drills

**Achilles Tendinopathy:** Eccentric heel drops (Alfredson protocol); 3 × 15 reps with knee straight (gastrocnemius) and bent (soleus); twice daily; load to mild pain tolerance.

## Part V: Special Areas

### Women's Health / Pelvic Floor (Ch. 23)
- **Kegel exercises:** Identify pelvic floor; 5–10 sec holds; 10–20 reps; multiple positions
- **Diastasis recti:** Avoid loaded trunk flexion; transverse abdominal activation priority
- **Prenatal/postnatal:** Pelvic floor + transverse abdominis; avoid supine after 1st trimester

### Pulmonary Rehabilitation (Ch. 25)
- Pursed-lip breathing; diaphragmatic breathing; incentive spirometry
- Airway clearance: Postural drainage; percussion; active cycle of breathing (ACBT)
- Aerobic conditioning: 60–80% symptom-limited peak HR; dyspnea monitoring (Borg breathlessness scale)
