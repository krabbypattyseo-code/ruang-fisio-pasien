# Rehab Science — Clinical Reference

Source: *Rehab Science* by Tom Walters, PT & Glen Cordoza (Victory Belt, 2023)

---

## Part I: Pain Science

### Pain Is Brain-Generated
- Pain comes **exclusively from the brain** — not from tissues or pain receptors
- Nociception (danger signals from tissue) ≠ pain; the brain decides whether to produce pain
- Phantom limb pain, imaging-pain mismatches, and visual cue experiments all confirm this
- Pain is a **protective output**, not a passive sensation

### Biopsychosocial (BPS) Model
| Component | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Bio** | Structure, posture, biomechanics, tissue load — the traditional PSB model. Most acute pain lives here. |
| **Psycho** | Thoughts, beliefs, emotions, perceived threat — critical for chronic pain. Brain can create pain from perceived danger alone. |
| **Social** | Social interaction, relationships, loneliness, cultural meaning of pain |

**PSB model alone is insufficient**, especially for chronic pain. Address all three components.

### Types of Pain
- **Acute pain** (< 3 months): direct response to tissue damage or threat; usually bio-driven
- **Chronic pain** (> 3 months): nervous system sensitization; no longer accurately reflects tissue state; requires addressing psychosocial factors

### Neuroplasticity and Sensitization
- Sensors become oversensitized through negative neuroplasticity → less stimulus needed to trigger pain
- Sensory sensitivity can develop within 2–3 days if the nervous system perceives danger
- **Graded exposure** reverses sensitization — gradually reintroduce threatening movements to desensitize sensors
- Chronic pain ≠ ongoing tissue damage; it reflects a sensitized nervous system

### Factors That Influence Pain
| Factor | Key Insight |
|---|---|
| **Imaging results** | Create nocebo effect; provoke anxiety and avoidance → worsen prognosis. 30–80% of pain-free people have disc bulges, 34% have rotator cuff tears on MRI. |
| **Stress** | Activates sympathetic NS; chronic stress sensitizes sensors and worsens pain |
| **Sleep** | Insufficient sleep impairs neuroplasticity and tissue remodeling; target 7–9 hrs/night |
| **Diet** | Inflammatory foods may sensitize nerve fibers; linked to chronic pain |
| **Posture** | Rarely causes pain at rest; "next posture is the best posture" — static positioning is the problem |
| **Social interaction** | Social withdrawal → depression → chronic pain spiral. Healthy social connections reduce pain. |
| **Culture/meaning** | Meaningful activities reduce perceived threat and pain (graded exposure is more effective when it involves valued activities) |
| **Anatomy** | Musculoskeletal system is adaptive; anatomical deviations don't automatically cause pain |

### How to Overcome Pain: Three Phases
| Phase | Goal | Strategies |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Reduce pain symptoms | Soft tissue mobilization, aerobic exercise, flexibility, avoid aggravating factors |
| **2** | Address impairments (weakness, ROM) | Mobility exercises (PROM→AAROM→AROM), isometric and eccentric resistance exercises, sensorimotor control |
| **3** | Build capacity and prevent flare-ups | Full ROM resistance training, progressive overload, return to sport |

**Active strategies** (exercise, movement) have better long-term evidence than **passive strategies** (manual therapy, heat/ice). Use passive strategies in Phase 1 to control symptoms, then transition to active.

## Part II: Injury Science

### Pain ≠ Injury
- You can have injury without pain (40–50% of population has labral tears, meniscus tears, disc herniations with no symptoms)
- You can have pain without injury (90–95% of low back pain diagnoses are non-specific)
- Use symptoms and function — not imaging findings — as the primary guide for rehab progress
- **Decoupling pain from injury** reduces fear and catastrophizing → faster recovery

### Injury Types and Grading

#### Muscle Strains
- Occur at musculotendinous junction during **eccentric contraction** (braking phase)
- **Grade I**: pain, minimal functional loss; **Grade II**: pain + ROM and strength deficits, swelling; **Grade III**: major tear, complete functional loss — may need surgery
- Sharp pain when loading; bruising/divot with Grade II–III

#### Tendon Injuries
- **Tendinopathy**: degeneration + pain, often from overuse (too much volume/frequency too fast)
- **Tendinosis**: recurrent tendinopathy → structural thickening on MRI
- **Rupture**: requires surgery in many cases
- Key rule: load tendons progressively — loading is the treatment, not rest

#### Ligament Sprains
- **Grade I**: mild; **Grade II**: stretching + partial tear; **Grade III**: full rupture
- Primary impairment = joint instability (especially Grade II–III)
- Focus: sensorimotor control before progressive loading

#### Cartilage Injuries (Labrum, Meniscus, Disc)
- Low vascularity → heals slowly or not at all
- But symptoms can resolve even if structural injury persists
- Degenerative tears: prefer rehab over surgery (equal outcomes, lower risk)
- Traumatic tears: may require surgery if persistent instability

#### Nerve Injuries
- Tension, compression, or laceration
- Signs: tingling, numbness, burning, shooting pain along nerve path, weakness
- **Peripheralization** (symptoms moving distally) = worsening; **Centralization** (symptoms moving toward spine) = improving
- "Pinched nerve" is usually inflammation, not mechanical compression

#### Bone Fractures
- Stress fractures: localized point tenderness, pain on load
- Simple fractures: 6–8 weeks; complex: up to 6 months
- Seek medical attention for suspected fractures

### Tissue Healing Timeline

| Tissue | Grade I | Grade II | Grade III/Severe |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Muscle strain** | 1–3 weeks | 4–12 weeks | 3–6 months (surgery) |
| **Tendinopathy** | 2–12 weeks | — | — |
| **Tendinosis** | 3–6 months | — | — |
| **Tendon rupture** | — | — | 3–12 months |
| **Ligament sprain** | 1–3 weeks | 4–6 weeks | 8 weeks–1 year (ACL: 9–12 months) |
| **Cartilage** | — | 3 months–2 years | May not heal fully |
| **Nerve irritation** | 4–6 weeks | — | — |
| **Nerve (severe)** | — | — | 3 months–1 year |
| **Bone (stress/simple)** | 6–8 weeks | — | — |

**Factors slowing healing:** severity, poor blood supply (cartilage/tendon), smoking, alcohol, poor sleep, high stress, deconditioning

### Three Phases of Tissue Healing
1. **Inflammation** (~72 hours): immune repair, sensitized region → pain management, unloaded movement only
2. **Maturation** (up to 3 weeks): tissue rebuilding → mobility, sensorimotor control, early resistance (isometric/eccentric)
3. **Remodeling** (4–6 weeks to 1 year+): full capacity rebuild → full ROM resistance training, progressive overload

## Part III: Rehab Protocols Framework

### Rehab Protocol Structure
Each protocol contains:
1. **Description** — tissue involved, mechanism, who it affects
2. **Signs & Symptoms** — location, quality of pain
3. **Aggravating Factors** — movements/activities to modify/avoid
4. **Prognosis** — recovery timeline; when to seek professional help
5. **Treatment Strategy** — rationale, medication considerations, surgery indicators
6. **Three-Phase Exercise Program**

### Protocols by Region
| Region | Conditions Covered |
|---|---|
| Head & Neck | Neck pain, whiplash, cervicogenic headache |
| Shoulder | Rotator cuff, labral tears, biceps tendinopathy, impingement, frozen shoulder |
| Elbow | Lateral/medial epicondylalgia (tennis/golfer's elbow), olecranon bursitis |
| Wrist & Hand | Carpal tunnel syndrome, De Quervain's, trigger finger |
| Back & Spine | Non-specific LBP, disc herniation, sciatica, radiculopathy, spinal stenosis |
| Hip | Hip impingement (FAI), labral tear, gluteal tendinopathy, trochanteric bursitis, OA |
| Knee | Patellofemoral pain, patellar/quadriceps tendinopathy, meniscus, ACL, OA |
| Ankle & Foot | Ankle sprain, Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis |

### Exercise Progression by Phase
| Phase | Exercise Types |
|---|---|
| **1** | Soft tissue mobilization (foam roll, massage ball, percussion gun), flexibility/stretching, aerobic exercise, PROM |
| **2** | AAROM/AROM mobility, isometric holds (30–45 sec × 4–5 reps — natural ibuprofen for tendons), eccentric contractions, sensorimotor/balance drills |
| **3** | Full ROM concentric + eccentric resistance training, progressive overload (load, volume, speed), return to sport integration |

**Exercise selection varies by injury type:**
- Muscle/tendon: skip or limit stretching in Phase 1 (can aggravate); lead with isometrics/eccentrics
- Ligament/cartilage: skip soft tissue mobilization; prioritize AROM and neuromuscular control early
- General pain/LBP/neck: start with soft tissue mobilization + stretching, then progress

### Pain Guidelines During Rehab
- Mild discomfort during exercise is acceptable
- Exercise is safe if baseline symptoms are **not flared up a few hours later**
- If symptoms worsen after exercise: reduce load, step back to previous phase
- Chronic pain flares ≠ reinjury — it's hypersensitivity, not tissue damage

### Acceptable Pain Level During Exercise
- Phase 1–2: ≤ 4–5/10 NRS; no lingering increase after exercise
- Phase 3: mild discomfort acceptable with progressive loading

## Red Flags — Refer to Doctor Immediately

| Red Flag | Concern |
|---|---|
| Recent significant trauma | Fracture or severe structural injury |
| Unrelenting resting/night pain | Serious pathology (infection, malignancy) |
| Saddle anesthesia | Cauda equina (surgical emergency) |
| Progressive lower extremity weakness | Nerve compromise |
| Bladder/bowel dysfunction | Nerve compromise or infection |
| History of cancer | Metastatic disease |
| Unexplained weight loss > 10 lbs in 3 months | Infection or cancer |
| Fever, chills, night sweats | Infection or cancer |
| Age > 50 + multiple red flags | Higher risk pathology |

Only ~1% of musculoskeletal issues involve serious pathology, but do not miss them.

---

## When to Refer to a PT or Doctor
- No improvement after 6 weeks of appropriate protocol
- Grade III muscle, tendon, or ligament injuries
- Progressive neurological symptoms (worsening weakness/numbness)
- Joint instability not improving with rehab
- Suspected cartilage injury with ongoing functional loss after 6–12 months
- Suspect fracture or serious pathology
