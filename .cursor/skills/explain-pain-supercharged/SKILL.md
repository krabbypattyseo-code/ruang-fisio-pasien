# Explain Pain Supercharged — Clinical Reference

Source: *Explain Pain Supercharged* by G. Lorimer Moseley & David S. Butler (Noigroup, 2017)

---

## Core Premise: The Grand Poobah Pain Theory (GPPT)

1. **Pain is always a brain output** — not a tissue readout. Pain emerges when the brain concludes that the body needs protecting, regardless of true tissue damage.
2. **Nociception ≠ Pain** — danger signals from tissues can exist without pain; pain can exist without nociception.
3. **Pain is protective** — it is one of many protective outputs (alongside sympathetic activation, immune responses, motor guarding, cognitive focus).
4. **Pain depends on perceived danger vs. perceived safety** — the balance of DIMs vs. SIMs determines whether pain is produced and how much.
5. **Pain is infinitomodal** — it can be influenced by anything that carries danger or safety relevance: thoughts, beliefs, movements, sounds, smells, context, relationships, memories.

## Biopsychosocial (BPS) Model

| Domain | Contributors |
|---|---|
| **Biological** | Nociceptor activation, inflammation, tissue damage, peripheral/central sensitisation, neuroimmune coupling |
| **Psychological** | Thoughts, beliefs, predictions, emotions, catastrophising, fear-avoidance, memory, attention, expectation |
| **Social** | Relationships, cultural meaning of pain, work environment, social withdrawal, family beliefs, media |

> The BPS model is the foundational lens for all Explain Pain work. Biological contributors are bodily events that activate nociceptors or drive tissue states outside homeostasis. Psychological contributors are brain-driven events (cognition, emotion, behaviour). Social contributors are interpersonal and environmental influences.

## Neurotags — Key Theoretical Framework

**Neurotag:** A distributed network of brain cells that collaborates to produce a specific output (pain, movement, emotion, immune response).

| Neurotag Type | Function |
|---|---|
| **Action neurotags** | Extend beyond the brain → produce outputs: pain, muscle activation, emotion, immune response |
| **Modulation neurotags** | Remain within the brain → influence action neurotags; represent implicit knowledge, sensory data, beliefs |
| **DIM neurotags** (Danger In Me) | Credible evidence of danger → increase protection, amplify pain |
| **SIM neurotags** (Safety In Me) | Credible evidence of safety → decrease protection, reduce pain |

**Neurotag properties:**
- **Mass**: more cells = more influence; nociception neurotags have high mass
- **Synaptic efficacy**: "neurons that fire together wire together" — practice and repetition strengthen tags
- **Precision**: specificity of activation; imprecise neurotags produce smudged, widespread pain/dysfunction

**Key implication:** Explain Pain works by building SIM neurotags and weakening DIM neurotags through education, metaphor, and conceptual change.

## DIMs and SIMs — The Protectometer

**DIM (Danger In Me):** Any cue or belief that the brain reads as credible evidence of danger to body tissue.

**SIM (Safety In Me):** Any cue or belief that the brain reads as credible evidence of safety.

**Examples:**

| DIMs (increase protection/pain) | SIMs (decrease protection/pain) |
|---|---|
| Catastrophic thoughts ("this is serious damage") | Understanding pain is protective, not always tissue-based |
| Scary diagnosis labels ("degenerative," "slipped disc") | Knowledge that anatomy tolerates load ("bones don't break from normal movement") |
| Fear of movement (kinesiophobia) | Graded exposure to feared movements |
| Sleep deprivation, stress, anxiety | Sleep, relaxation, social connection |
| Over-reliance on passive treatments | Active self-management strategies |
| Well-meaning but fear-inducing clinician language | Clinician communication that is reassuring and evidence-based |
| "Danger" in the workplace / social environment | Supportive relationships, returning to meaningful activity |

**Clinical tool:** The Protectometer maps a patient's DIMs and SIMs visually. The balance between them determines the "protection level" and hence pain output.

## Pain Biology: Key Mechanisms

### A. Peripheral: Nociception and Sensitisation
- Nociceptors are **danger detectors**, not pain receptors
- Ion channels (TRP family, Nav1.7, Nav1.8, ASICs) open when a potential threat is detected
- **Peripheral sensitisation:** Local inflammation lowers nociceptor threshold → allodynia and hyperalgesia at the injury site
- Key mediators: TNFα, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, bradykinin, prostaglandins, NGF
- "Power peptides" (CGRP, substance P, glutamate) released by nociceptors → facilitate healing and vasodilation (nociceptors are not just danger messengers — they also nurture tissue repair)

### B. Dorsal Horn: Spinal Processing
- First synapse is NOT the first processing site — DRG (dorsal root ganglion) also processes danger signals with neuroimmune interactions
- **Central sensitisation (spinal):** Wind-up, LTP in spinal nociceptors → wider receptive fields, lower thresholds, allodynia/hyperalgesia
- Descending facilitation (from brainstem) can amplify spinal nociceptor activity if the brain perceives more danger than signals suggest

### C. Brain: Central Sensitisation and Cortical Changes
- No single "pain centre" — pain requires **distributed brain activation** (insular cortex, anterior cingulate, somatosensory cortex, amygdala, prefrontal cortex, and more)
- With persistent pain: pain neurotag gains **mass and synaptic efficacy** → less input needed to produce pain
- **Cortical body matrix disruption:** Smudged body maps, poor tactile acuity, impaired motor control, body perception disturbances
- **Imprecision hypothesis:** Overlapping neurotags for pain and related body parts → widespread, unpredictable pain distribution

### D. Neuroimmune Coupling
- Immune cells (microglia, astrocytes, T cells) are embedded in the nervous system and modulate nociception
- TLR4 activation (by DAMPs, PAMPs, xenobiotics) lowers pain threshold at the spinal level
- Pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNFα) facilitate long-term potentiation (LTP) in the dorsal horn → contribute to sensitisation
- **Creating a DIM may be pro-inflammatory; creating a SIM may be anti-inflammatory** (early evidence from animal studies)

### Mechanisms Cheat Sheet
| Feature | Peripheral Sensitisation | Central Sensitisation |
|---|---|---|
| Location | Nociceptors at injury site | Spinal cord + brain |
| Cause | Local inflammation, tissue damage | Persistent nociceptive input, psychological factors |
| Result | Allodynia/hyperalgesia at injury site | Widespread pain, allodynia at distant sites, spontaneous pain |
| Reversibility | Resolves with inflammation | Slower; requires BPS intervention |

## Central Sensitisation — Clinical Indicators

Suspect central sensitisation when:
- Pain distribution has **spread** since onset (regional, hemi-body, or whole-body)
- **Mirror pain** (contralateral side)
- **Allodynia**: light touch (brush) provokes pain
- **Temporal summation**: repeated stimuli produce escalating pain
- Flare-ups unpredictable or triggered by non-mechanical cues (stress, emotion, weather)
- Flare-ups occur **2–10 days after** inciting event
- **Reduced DNIC** (descending noxious inhibitory control) on QST
- Poor tactile acuity in painful area
- Questionnaires: high scores on PCS (catastrophising), HADS (anxiety/depression), FABQ (fear-avoidance)

## The Evidence for Explain Pain

- ~20 RCTs demonstrate Explain Pain improves: pain knowledge, catastrophising, participation in active rehabilitation, pain thresholds, and physical performance
- **NNT for 50% pain/disability reduction at 6 months ≈ 4** (equal to or better than other current approaches)
- **NNH: none reported** in clinical trials
- Level 1 evidence: improves pain knowledge, catastrophising, and rehabilitation participation
- Level 2 evidence: Explain Pain-based rehabilitation improves pain, disability, work status, and quality of life
- Explain Pain + exercise is more effective than either alone
- Explaining anatomy/biomechanics ("back school") can worsen outcomes by reinforcing DIMs

## Conceptual Change: How to Teach Pain

### Four Variables Affecting Educational Outcome

| Variable | What It Means Clinically |
|---|---|
| **Learner** | Existing beliefs (DIMs/SIMs), motivation, pain literacy, health literacy, learning style, disability level, medication effects on cognition |
| **Deliverer** | Knowledge of pain science, teaching skills, ability to listen, own biases, communication style |
| **Message** | Target Concepts, metaphors used, comprehensibility, plausibility, coherence, emotional resonance |
| **Context** | Clinic environment, time, group vs. individual, social support, access to digital media |

### Message Quality Requirements
A persuasive educational message must be:
1. **Comprehensible** — appropriate to learner's literacy level; use metaphor and story to make biology accessible
2. **Plausible** — backed by evidence; multiple consistent voices; alternatives provided
3. **Coherent** — all parts fit together without contradiction
4. **Compelling** — novel, slightly emotional, personally relevant, well-timed

### Deep vs. Superficial Learning
- **Superficial:** Patient can recall facts but doesn't apply them; behaviour unchanged
- **Deep:** Patient extrapolates to new situations, self-generates SIMs, uses new language, functions differently
- Aim for deep learning: catchy but meaningful slogans, fridge magnets with Target Concepts, drawing, multimedia, storytelling

### Misconception Types
| Type | Description | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Missing bits** | Gaps in knowledge | Add new information |
| **Single grain** | One strong, isolated, incorrect belief | Provide alternative story for that specific belief |
| **Sandcastle** | Multiple loosely held misconceptions | Gradually challenge and rebuild |
| **Sandstone** | Deeply entrenched, emotionally charged misconceptions | Long-term relationship; incremental change; use incoherence as opportunity |

## Eight Great Questions (EP Assessment)

Before designing a curriculum, answer these:

1. **Does the learner really want to know about pain and science?** (If not, address this as a Target Concept first)
2. **How does the learner like to learn?** (visual, verbal, group, one-on-one, digital)
3. **Does the learner have an impaired ability to learn?** (pain-related cognitive effects, medication, literacy)
4. **Does the learner have access to digital media?**
5. **Where does the learner currently seek health knowledge?** (Prof. Google? Well-meaning friends? Assess source quality)
6. **What is the learner's current pain literacy?** (Most default to tissue-damage model; NPQ can assess)
7. **What kind of misconception does the learner hold?** (missing bits, sandcastle, sandstone)
8. **What are the relevant Target Concepts for this learner?** (Identify from clinical assessment + DIM/SIM analysis)

## Target Concepts (Common Examples)

| Target Concept | Key Message |
|---|---|
| Pain is a protective output | Pain comes from the brain concluding the body needs protection — not from tissue damage itself |
| We are bioplastic | The brain and nervous system can change — for better or worse |
| Pain is one of many protective outputs | Sympathetic activation, immune changes, guarding, anxiety are all part of protection |
| Pain depends on the balance of DIMs and SIMs | Anything that credibly signals danger increases pain; safety signals reduce it |
| Nociception ≠ pain | You can have injury without pain, and pain without injury |
| Sensitivity can change | Central sensitisation explains why pain can spread, become unpredictable, and persist beyond tissue healing |
| Movement is a SIM | Safe, graded movement reduces protection and pain — "motion is lotion" |
| Knowledge itself reduces pain | Understanding pain biology changes brain processing; education is treatment |
| Clinician language matters | Words like "degeneration," "instability," and "slipped disc" are DIMs |

## Metaphors and Therapeutic Narratives

Metaphors are powerful because they engage existing neurotags (familiar → novel connection). Use them to make biology comprehensible.

**Categories of metaphors:**
- **Structural** — "The alarm system is oversensitive, not the structure itself"
- **Orientational** — "You've been heading downward, now we turn around"
- **Invasive** — "The pain has invaded your life — let's take back territory"
- **Ontological** — Giving pain a character or entity: "Your overprotective guard"
- **Diagnostic** — "It's not a design flaw, it's a software glitch"
- **Prognostic** — "The body is bioplastic — it rewires with the right input"

**Tips for metaphor use:**
- Use familiar objects/experiences for the patient
- Pair metaphors with drawing, visuals, or multimedia
- Multiple metaphors for the same Target Concept = redundancy = stronger neurotag
- Avoid metaphors that reinforce structural fragility (DIM metaphors)

**High-impact clinical phrases:**
- "Motion is lotion" (movement as SIM)
- "Don't flare up. But don't freak out if you do." (normalise flares)
- "Pain is your brain's best guess about danger — and sometimes it gets it wrong"
- "A little pain with exercise means your sensors are working, not that you're causing damage"

## EP Curriculum Structure

A good Explain Pain curriculum has four aspects:
1. **Body of knowledge** — the science and concepts to transmit
2. **Product** — measurable objectives and outcomes (use NPQ before and after)
3. **Process** — dynamic interaction between learner, deliverer, message, context
4. **Practice** — the patient lives the curriculum beyond sessions; it becomes a way of life

**Curriculum development steps:**
1. Extract demographic/practical data (learner, deliverer, context, time)
2. State aims (what the deliverer will do) and objectives (what the learner will do)
3. Define Target Concepts per patient/group
4. Populate delivery methods, resources, metaphors aligned to each Target Concept
5. Assess formatively (during sessions) and summatively (NPQ, questionnaires, functional changes)

**Measurement tools:**
- **NPQ** (Neurophysiology of Pain Questionnaire) — before and after every EP programme
- **PCS** (Pain Catastrophizing Scale)
- **HADS** (Hospital Anxiety & Depression Scale)
- **FABQ** (Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire)
- **IES-R** (Impact of Event Scale) — for post-traumatic pain states
- **Functional outcomes** — SLR, physical performance, return to work/activity
