# Anatomy Trains — Myofascial Meridians Reference

Source: *Anatomy Trains* by Thomas W. Myers, PT (4th ed., Elsevier 2021)

---

## Core Concept

Muscles do not act in isolation. Beyond their individual origin-to-insertion function, they transmit force through **myofascial continuities** — interlinked chains of muscle and connective tissue called **myofascial meridians** (Anatomy Trains).

**Key clinical insight:** Pain in one area often originates from a "silent" restriction somewhere else along the same line. Treat the whole chain, not just the symptom site.

**Pattern recognition** — not the technique used — is the primary skill.

---

## The 12 Myofascial Lines

### 1. Superficial Back Line (SBL)
**Route:** Plantar fascia/toe phalanges → calcaneus → gastrocnemius/Achilles tendon → hamstrings → sacrotuberous ligament → sacral fascia/erector spinae → epicranial fascia (galea aponeurotica) → supraorbital ridge (brow)

**Postural function:** Supports full upright extension; prevents collapse into fetal flexion (except at knee, where it creates flexion)

**Movement function:** Creates extension and hyperextension throughout body

**Common compensation patterns:** Ankle dorsiflexion limitation, knee hyperextension, hamstring shortness, anterior pelvic shift, lumbar lordosis, suboccipital limitation, upper cervical hyperextension, occiput anterior shift on atlas

**Clinical note:** Restriction in plantar fascia often correlates with tight hamstrings and upper cervical restriction via SBL. Suboccipitals (rectus/obliquus capitis) are the "functional centerpiece" — they have ~50× more muscle spindles per gram than gluteus maximus. Neck and eye movement patterns drive the entire SBL.

**Key stretches:** Forward bend (seated, standing), Downward Dog, Plow

---

### 2. Superficial Front Line (SFL)
**Route:** Toe extensors/anterior crural compartment → tibialis anterior → subpatellar tendon/quadriceps → rectus abdominis → sternalis/sternocostal fascia → SCM → mastoid process (scalp loop)

**Postural function:** Protects vulnerable anterior structures; flexes trunk and neck

**Movement function:** Trunk flexion, hip flexion, ankle dorsiflexion, neck extension

**Common compensation patterns:** Forward head posture (SCM pulling down on skull), depressed ribs and restricted breathing, anterior pelvic tilt, flat lumbar curve

**Critical SFL–SBL relationship:**
- When SBL is "locked short" (military posture), SFL is "locked long" (eccentrically loaded = painful)
- When SFL is locked short (collapsed/burdened posture), SBL becomes overloaded
- **Treat the locked short (silent) line, not just the locked long (noisy) one**
- Working only the painful back without releasing the SFL gives only temporary relief

**Startle response = SFL shortening:** All negative emotion expressed as shortening of the SFL — mastoid brought toward pubic bone. Chronic startle posture restricts breathing and depresses rib cage.

---

### 3. Lateral Line (LL)
**Route:** Fibularis longus (1st metatarsal base) + fibularis brevis (5th metatarsal base) → lateral crural compartment → fibular head → anterior ligament of fibular head → iliotibial tract (ITT) → TFL + gluteus medius + superior gluteus maximus → iliac crest → external/internal abdominal obliques → ribs/intercostals → 1st and 2nd ribs → SCM/splenius capitis → mastoid/occiput

**Postural function:** Balances front–back and left–right; mediates forces between all other superficial lines; outer stabilizing system for trunk and legs

**Movement function:** Lateral flexion, hip abduction, foot eversion; acts as brake for rotational movement; watch-spring for walking

**Common compensation patterns:** Ankle pronation/supination, genu varus/valgus, lumbar side-bend, rib cage lateral shift, head forward posture

**Important:** ITT cannot be lengthened by stretching/foam rolling — it is too dense. Work to hydrate, improve proprio/interoception, and ease glide on underlying vastus lateralis.

**Deep Lateral Line concomitants (technically part of DFL):** Quadratus lumborum (lateral flexion, lumbar) + scalenes (lateral flexion, cervical) — always address alongside LL work.

**LL and walking:** Intercostals function like a watch-spring, modulating trunk rotation with each step. Restriction here forces arms to compensate.

---

### 4. Spiral Line (SL)
**Route (bilateral helix):** Splenius capitis/cervicis (occiput/C1–C3) → contralateral rhomboids → serratus anterior → external oblique → internal oblique (crossing midline) → TFL → iliotibial tract → tibialis anterior → fibularis longus (under foot arch) → biceps femoris → sacrotuberous ligament → erector spinae → back to splenius on opposite side

**Postural function:** Wraps the body in two helixes, maintaining balance in all planes; stabilizes and maintains the arch of the foot; helps control knee tracking

**Movement function:** Creates and controls rotation; responsible for rotational movement of trunk

**Common clinical patterns:** Rotational or torsional postural patterns; scoliosis; knee tracking issues (medial or lateral tibial rotation); foot arch problems

**Note:** The Spiral Line includes Vleeming's Posterior Longitudinal Sling and Dart's double-spiral trunk muscles.

---

### 5. Arm Lines (4 lines)

| Line | Axial Origin | Arm Track | Distal End |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Superficial Front Arm Line (SFAL)** | Pec minor/clavipectoral fascia | Biceps brachii | Radial periosteum / thumb |
| **Deep Front Arm Line (DFAL)** | Pec minor → coracobrachialis | Brachialis | Thenar muscles/thumb |
| **Superficial Back Arm Line (SBAL)** | Trapezius/deltoid | Lateral epicondyle | Extensor group / little finger |
| **Deep Back Arm Line (DBAL)** | Rhomboids/rotator cuff | Triceps | Ulnar periosteum / little finger |

**Clinical use:** Shoulder and arm problems often require addressing axial attachments (rhomboids, pec minor, trapezius). Deep lines involve deeper structures and stabilize the shoulder joint.

---

### 6. Functional Lines (3 lines)

**Back Functional Line (BFL):**  
Latissimus dorsi → thoracolumbar fascia → sacral fascia → contralateral gluteus maximus → vastus lateralis → patella → tibial tuberosity

**Front Functional Line (FFL):**  
Pectoralis major (lower) → 5th–6th rib → semilunar line/rectus sheath → pubic tubercle → contralateral adductor longus → linea aspera

**Ipsilateral Functional Line (IFL):**  
Latissimus dorsi (lateral) → lower ribs 10–12 → external oblique → ASIS → sartorius → medial tibial condyle

**Function:** All Functional Lines connect the arm girdle to the opposite (or ipsilateral) leg girdle — lengthening the lever arm for power and transferring momentum across the body. Essential for throwing, kicking, contralateral gait balance.

**Clinical note:** Rotator cuff injuries in throwing athletes often result from insufficient BFL strength/timing. Reinforce the whole BFL rather than treating only the small shoulder muscles.

---

### 7. Deep Front Line (DFL)
**Route:** Underside of foot (intrinsics/FHL/FDL) → tibialis posterior → popliteus → knee capsule → adductors/medial thigh → pelvic floor → iliopsoas/iliacus → lumbar spine (anterior) + hip (anterior and posterior via separate tracks) → diaphragm → pericardium → mediastinal fascia → scalenes → deep cervical flexors (longus colli/capitis) → cranial base

**Postural function:** The body's myofascial "core" — deepest, most central stabilizing line; inner stabilizing system (contrast with LL as outer stabilizer)

**Movement function:** Hip and trunk stability; breath support; cervical and lumbar deep stabilization

**Key structures:** Psoas (hip flexion, lateral flexion, lumbar stabilization), pelvic floor, diaphragm, deep cervical flexors, mediastinum

**Clinical note:** DFL dysfunction underlies many chronic low back pain patterns, pelvic floor issues, breathing restriction, and deep cervical instability. The psoas connects the legs to the spine via the DFL.

## BodyReading — Structural Assessment (Ch. 11)

### Principles
- Assess the **individual's own relationships** — not against an ideal norm
- Look for lines that are "locked short" vs "locked long" (see SFL–SBL section)
- Pattern recognition across all planes and lines

### Assessment Views

| View | Primary Lines Assessed |
|---|---|
| Anterior | LL (bilateral symmetry), SFL, FFL |
| Posterior | SBL, LL, Spiral Line, BFL |
| Lateral | SBL vs SFL balance, DFL |
| Gait | All lines; especially LL, Spiral, Functional |

### Summary Lateral 'X' Assessment
From lateral view: one leg of X from C7 spinous process to pubic bone, other from sternal notch to sacral apex. If one leg is significantly shorter → pattern of compensation indicated.

### Common Postural Patterns

| Pattern | Shortened/Locked Short | Lengthened/Locked Long |
|---|---|---|
| Military/anterior pelvic shift | SBL (middle) | SFL (front hip/abs) |
| Forward head posture | SFL (SCM pulling down), scalenes | SBL (upper cervical/suboccipital) |
| Collapsed/burdened posture | SFL (front trunk) | SBL |
| Scoliosis/rotation | Spiral Line (one side) | Spiral Line (opposite) |
| Lateral trunk shift | LL (one side) | LL (opposite) |
| Head forward + LL | LL shoulder involvement with head stability | — |

## Key Clinical Principles

### 1. Treat the Silent, Not Just the Noisy
- **Eccentrically loaded / locked long** muscles = painful, active trigger points ("noisy")
- **Concentrically loaded / locked short** muscles = often silent but are the root cause
- Treating only the painful area gives temporary relief; release the locked short area for lasting change

### 2. Myofascial Continuity
- Strain is transmitted along lines → symptom site ≠ source site
- A restriction in the plantar fascia can drive hamstring tightness and suboccipital pain (all SBL)
- A frozen shoulder may be addressed via ribs, hip, or neck

### 3. Global Before Local
- Assess the whole body pattern first
- Balance the lines, then work specific areas
- Unbalanced lines lead to joint overload and compensatory patterns

### 4. Expresses and Locals
- **Express muscles** = polyarticular (cross multiple joints), govern overall line direction
- **Local muscles** = monoarticular (single joint), govern postural "set"
- Chronic patterns are more often maintained by the **locals** (deep, single-joint) than the expresses
- Example: anterior pelvic tilt → treat iliacus/pectineus (locals), not just rectus femoris/sartorius (expresses)

### 5. Stations and Roundhouses
- **Stations** = bony attachments where lines tack down; also where restrictions accumulate
- **Roundhouses** = where multiple lines converge (e.g., ASIS, pubic bone) → high clinical importance
- Restriction at a station affects both tracks on either side of it

## Practical Application

### Manual Therapy Direction
When working a line:
- Move tissue in the direction of **lengthening** a locked short area
- Work with client's active movement (eccentric lengthening) when possible
- Superficial fibers communicate more along the line than deep fibers

### Spinal Rule (SBL work)
- Spinous processes that **protrude** (mountains) = spread tissue toward them (medially)
- Spinous processes that are **buried** (valleys) = spread tissue away laterally

### Movement Education
- Teach clients to engage the full line, not just local muscles
- Functional lines: have client lift one arm and opposite leg simultaneously (BFL) to assess coordination
- Wave motions through the spine assess and treat SBL restrictions
- Dynamic movement through range of motion integrates lines better than passive stretching
