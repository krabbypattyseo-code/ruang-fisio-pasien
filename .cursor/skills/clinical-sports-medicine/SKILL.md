## Part A: Fundamental Principles

### Pain Science (Ch. 5 — Lorimer Moseley)

**Core principle:** Pain is a protective output of the brain — not a direct measure of tissue damage.

- **Nociception ≠ Pain**: Nociceptors detect danger signals, but pain only occurs if the brain decides protection is needed
- **Peripheral sensitisation**: Nociceptors become more sensitive at the injury site (local, time-limited)
- **Central sensitisation**: Spinal nociceptors become sensitised, widening pain area and reducing threshold
- **Cortical changes**: In persistent pain, the brain itself adapts → pain becomes more generalised, less linked to tissue state
- **Brain corrects the spinal cord**: Descending modulation can be facilitatory (amplifies danger) or inhibitory — context, beliefs, and emotions directly modulate spinal nociception

**Clinical implications:**
- The same mechanical input can hurt one day and not the next
- Pain may exist with no identifiable structural cause
- Imaging findings rarely correlate with pain severity or chronicity
- Treat the whole person (biopsychosocial), not just the tissue

### Pain Classification (Ch. 6 — Ebonie Rio)

| Type | Characteristics | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Input-dominated** | Clear nociceptive contribution; consistent with activity and movement; settles as injury heals | Ankle sprain, tendinopathy |
| **Referred pain (somatic)** | Indistinct borders; diffuse, deep, dull; can move; does not follow dermatomes | Facet joint, trigger point referral |
| **Radicular pain** | Sharp, shoots in narrow band; follows dermatome; may have neurological signs | Disc herniation, nerve root compression |
| **Neuropathic pain** | Burning, shooting, hypersensitivity; spontaneous; may have motor deficit | Peripheral nerve damage, CRPS |
| **Centrally dominated** | Widespread, unpredictable, disproportionate to activity; psychosocial drivers prominent | Fibromyalgia, central sensitisation |

## Acute Injuries (Ch. 3)

### Tissue-Based Classification

**Bone:** Fracture (complete, stress) → Periosteal contusion → Chondral/osteochondral injury

**Ligament sprains — grading:**
| Grade | Pathology | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| I | Few fibres torn | Local tenderness; minimal swelling; normal end-feel (may be painful) |
| II | Considerable fibres torn | Swelling; increased laxity; definite end-point; moderate function loss |
| III | Complete rupture | Audible pop; haemarthrosis; no end-point; significant instability |

**Muscle strains:**
- Most common: hamstring (76–87% biceps femoris), quadriceps, gastrocnemius
- Mechanism: eccentric overload at musculotendinous junction
- **Grade 0**: Clinical syndrome, no imaging evidence
- **Grade I**: Pain, minimal strength loss
- **Grade II**: Pain + swelling + strength deficit
- **Grade III**: Complete tear — surgery often required
- Strains involving intramuscular/central tendon: significantly longer recovery (72 vs 21 days median for biceps femoris with vs without central tendon disruption)

**Tendon tear/rupture:** Acute, not overuse — distinct from tendinopathy

**Joint dislocation:** Reduce promptly after neurovascular screen; X-ray post-reduction; early protected mobilisation; train active/neural subsystems

**Cartilage:** Fibrocartilage (meniscus, labrum, TFCC) — clicking, locking, giving way; limited healing in avascular zones

## Overuse Injuries (Ch. 4)

### Tendinopathy — Cook-Purdam Continuum

| Stage | Pathology | Clinical / Imaging |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Reactive** | Non-inflammatory tendon cell activation; proteoglycan increase; swelling reduces stress | Acute overload (young); fusiform swelling on US/MRI |
| **2. Tendon dysrepair** | Matrix breakdown; collagen disorganisation; early vessel/nerve ingrowth | Overloaded tendon (young-adult); hypoechoic on US; increased vascularity |
| **3. Degenerative** | Apoptosis; disordered collagen; large vessels; structurally compromised | Older/chronic; nodular thickening; intratendinous signal on MRI |
| **Reactive on degenerative** | Normal tendon tissue reacts to overload adjacent to degenerate area | Risk of rupture if degenerative load is extensive |

**Clinical presentation:** Pain after exercise or next morning on rising; "warm up and improve" pattern; local tenderness ± thickening

**Tendinopathy rehab (lower limb — Cook & Purdam):**
1. **Isometrics** — 4 × 45 sec at 70% MVC; immediate pain relief; normalises cortical inhibition; use until pain stable
2. **Isotonic (slow heavy)** — concentric + eccentric; 3-sec concentric, 4-sec eccentric; target kinetic chain muscles; up to 12 weeks
3. **Energy storage** — faster eccentric with slow concentric; 2–3×/week; controlled volume
4. **Sport-specific energy storage and release** — replaces stage 3; 2–3×/week; progress to training environment then competition

> Continue isotonic exercises ≥2×/week throughout entire program and after return to sport.

### Bone Stress Injuries (BSI)

**Continuum:** Bone strain → stress reaction → stress fracture → complete fracture

**Low-risk vs high-risk sites:**
| Low Risk | High Risk |
|---|---|
| Posteromedial tibia, fibula, femoral shaft, metatarsals 2–4, pelvis, calcaneus | Femoral neck, anterior tibial cortex, medial malleolus, navicular, 5th metatarsal base, sesamoids |

**Imaging of choice:** MRI (sensitive + specific, no radiation)  
MRI grades 1–4 → return-to-sport times: Grade 1 ≈ 11 wk, Grade 2 ≈ 13 wk, Grade 3 ≈ 19 wk, Grade 4 ≈ 32 wk

**Management — low-risk BSI:**
- Phase 1: Pain-free ADLs; activity modification; conditioning (cycling, swimming, deep-water running); address risk factors (nutrition, vitamin D, load history)
- Phase 2: Graduated return to running program (Stage 0: pain-free walking → Stage 1: walk/jog intervals at 50% → Stage 2: progressive pace → Stage 3: consecutive run days)

## Clinical Assessment (Ch. 15)

**Diagnosis keys:** History → physical exam → differential → imaging (only when needed)

**History essentials:**
- Exact injury mechanism and onset circumstances
- Pain quality, location, radiation, aggravating/easing factors
- Training load history (recent changes in volume/intensity)
- Equipment, technique, surface changes
- Previous similar injuries
- Psychological and nutritional factors

**Physical exam priorities:**
- Examine both sides
- Assess local tissues + referred pain + neural mechanosensitivity + spine
- Biomechanical and functional testing
- Attempt to reproduce patient's symptoms

**Diagnostic imaging habits:**
- Imaging after history + exam, not before
- MRI: best for soft tissue (tendon, cartilage, labrum, nerve)
- X-ray: first line for fractures
- Ultrasound: real-time dynamic assessment; operator-dependent
- CT: cortical fracture lines (stress fractures, pars); less soft tissue detail
- Bone scan: high sensitivity, poor specificity

## Treatment (Ch. 17)

### Acute Injury Management — POLICE
| Letter | Action |
|---|---|
| **P**rotection | Protect from further injury — bracing, crutches, relative rest |
| **O**ptimal Loading | Early, graded loading stimulates repair (mechanotherapy); avoid complete rest beyond acute phase |
| **I**ce | Reduce pain and swelling — 15–20 min, not directly on skin |
| **C**ompression | Limit swelling — bandage distal to proximal |
| **E**levation | Above heart level |

> **Do no HARM:** Heat, Alcohol, Running, Massage — avoid in acute phase

### Exercise-Based Treatments
- **Therapeutic (not "corrective") exercise**: Mechanotherapy stimulates repair; graded load improves tissue capacity
- **Motor control training**: Retrain neuromuscular patterns; corrects kinetic chain deficits
- **Exercise-induced hypoalgesia**: Aerobic exercise raises pain threshold — use as pain management tool

### Manual Therapy
- Joint mobilisation/manipulation: Short-term pain relief, improved ROM
- Soft tissue therapy: Reduce muscle tension; trigger point treatment; reduce neural mechanosensitivity
- Dry needling: Trigger point inactivation; neurophysiological mechanisms
- Neurodynamic techniques: For nerve-related symptoms with adverse neural mechanosensitivity

### Injections
- **Corticosteroids**: Short-term pain relief (weeks); risk of tendon weakening with repeated use; avoid direct tendon injection
- **PRP**: Some evidence in tendinopathy (patellar, lateral elbow); less for other conditions
- **NSAIDs**: Short-term use for acute inflammatory phase; avoid long-term (impairs healing, GI risk)

## Rehabilitation Principles (Ch. 18)

**4 Phases of Rehabilitation:**
| Phase | Goal | Key Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Acute** | Pain and swelling control; protect tissue | POLICE, passive ROM, gentle isometrics, hydrotherapy |
| **2. Restore ADL** | Regain full ROM; reduce impairments; normal movement patterns | Active mobility, progressive loading, neuromuscular re-education |
| **3. Return to sport** | Sport-specific strength, power, endurance; technical drills | Plyometrics, sport-specific movement, progressive running/throwing program |
| **4. Prevention of re-injury** | Identify and address contributing factors; maintenance program | Load management, prehab exercises, monitoring |

**Goal setting:** Set clear functional milestones for each phase — don't use time alone to progress.

## Return to Play (Ch. 19 — StARRT Framework)

**Strategic Assessment of Risk and Risk Tolerance (StARRT):**
1. **Tissue health** — Is the tissue healed sufficiently?
2. **Tissue stresses** — What loads will the sport place on the tissue?
3. **Risk tolerance modifiers** — Sport importance to athlete; competition level; available mitigation (taping, bracing)

**Decision makers:** Clinician + athlete + coach + team management (multidisciplinary consensus)

**Key principle:** Return-to-play is a medical and athlete-centred decision, not solely a time-based milestone.

## Conditions That Masquerade as Sports Injuries (Ch. 7)

Always consider when: atypical history; failure to progress as expected; night pain; constitutional symptoms

| Category | Key Conditions |
|---|---|
| Bone/soft tissue tumours | Osteosarcoma, osteoid osteoma (night pain + aspirin relief), synovial sarcoma, ganglion |
| Rheumatological | Ankylosing spondylitis, psoriatic enthesopathy, reactive arthritis, RA, inflammatory monoarthritis |
| Muscle disorders | Dermatomyositis, polymyositis, limb girdle dystrophy |
| Endocrine | Hypothyroidism (proximal weakness, fibromyalgia-like), hyperparathyroidism, diabetes (adhesive capsulitis) |
| Vascular | DVT, axillary vein thrombosis, popliteal artery entrapment, peripheral vascular disease |
| Infection | Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, Lyme disease |
| Pain syndromes | CRPS Type I, fibromyalgia |

**Red flags for malignancy/infection:** Night pain, fever, weight loss, malaise, age >50, history of cancer

## Sports Concussion (Ch. 20)

**Definition:** Traumatic brain injury caused by biomechanical forces; results in neurological dysfunction; typically self-limiting; no structural brain injury on imaging.

**On-field management:**
1. Remove from play immediately — "if in doubt, sit them out"
2. Use Sport Concussion Assessment Tool (SCAT5)
3. Assess for cervical spine injury
4. Serial observation — symptoms may evolve

**Graduated Return to Sport (6 steps):**
1. Symptom-limited activity (daily activities)
2. Light aerobic exercise (no resistance)
3. Sport-specific exercise
4. Non-contact training drills
5. Full contact practice (medical clearance required)
6. Return to competition

**Premature RTP risk:** Second impact syndrome (rare but catastrophic); prolonged recovery; post-concussion syndrome

## Kinetic Chain Principle

Every athletic movement involves proximal-to-distal sequencing. A deficit anywhere in the chain causes distal links to compensate ("catch-up"), increasing injury risk and impairing performance.

- Always assess the full kinetic chain, not just the symptomatic site
- A lumbar restriction → rotator cuff overload in throwers
- Hip abductor weakness → patellofemoral or IT band problems
- Treat the silent dysfunction, not just the painful symptom
