# Science of Running — Clinical Reference

Source: *Science of Running* by Chris Napier, PT, PhD (DK, 2020)

---

## Running Gait Cycle

Four key events per stride:

| Phase | Key Actions |
|---|---|
| **Initial Contact** | Most runners rearfoot strike; hip flexed, knee slightly flexed, ankle dorsiflexed. Landing position relative to COM affects braking force. |
| **Midstance** | Vertical GRF at maximum; quads & calves contract eccentrically to absorb impact; Achilles tendon stretches and stores elastic energy. COM at its lowest. |
| **Toe-Off** | Hip/knee extend; ankle plantarflexes; Achilles recoils returning stored energy; glutes, quads, proximal hamstrings, and calves contract concentrically. |
| **Swing** | Leg swings from behind to ahead of COM; most energy for swing comes from elastic recoil of muscles/tendons from stance. |

**Ground Reaction Force (GRF):** Knee sustains up to 10× body weight during running.

## Evidence-Based Myth Busting

| Myth | Evidence-Based Fact |
|---|---|
| Running causes knee osteoarthritis | Recreational running can *protect* against knee OA; even with OA, running may improve symptoms |
| Must static-stretch before running to prevent injury | Static stretching does **not** reduce injury risk and can decrease performance; use dynamic warm-up instead |
| Shoe type determines injury risk | Evidence does not support any particular shoe type preventing injury; avoid rapid shoe changes; monitor training load |
| High-rep low-resistance training builds running strength | Heavy resistance training (≥6 weeks, twice weekly) improves running performance and reduces injury risk |
| Forefoot strike prevents injury and improves economy | Type of injury may differ by footstrike, but **overall injury incidence is the same** for rearfoot vs forefoot strikers |

## Common Running Injuries

### Patellofemoral Pain ("Runner's Knee")
**Location:** Around, behind, or under patella  
**Common Causes:** Rapid training load increase; increased hip adduction; harder surfaces; downhill running  
**Treatment:**
- Short-term: taping, bracing, off-the-shelf orthotics; reduce training load
- Gait retraining if biomechanical risk factor present
- Strength: hip & thigh muscles (see: TFL ball release p.92, hip hike p.118, step down p.120, step up p.122, standing hip rotation p.126, single-leg ball squat p.136)

**Return to Training:** Use pain as guide; cross-train (bike/pool); softer surfaces; avoid hills initially; increase load gradually

---

### Achilles Tendinopathy
**Location:** Along tendon mid-portion or at calcaneal insertion  
**Common Causes:** Rapid training load increase (distance, frequency, or intensity); footwear changes; harder terrain  
**Key Clinical Note:** Pain may take up to 24 hours to manifest after overloading — pain is not always a reliable immediate indicator  
**Treatment:**
- Anti-inflammatory medication for pain relief if needed
- Reduce training load; higher-heeled shoes or heel wedges to unload tendon
- Dynamic calf stretches + heavy eccentric/strength training: calf stretch p.82, heel drop p.108, single-leg hop p.154

**Return to Training:** If caught early, tendon may settle in 5–10 days; avoid speedwork and uphill running until healed

---

### Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome / MTSS ("Shin Splints")
**Location:** Diffuse pain along medial (inner) border of tibia, ≥5 cm  
**Common Causes:** Impact on harder/cambered surfaces; rapid training increase; increased pronation; foot abduction; narrow step width; cadence <170 steps/min  
**Treatment:**
- Reduce training load; graded loading exposure
- Gait retraining for biomechanical risk factors
- Strength: soleus and tibialis posterior (heel drop p.108, ankle turn out p.112, ankle turn in p.114)

**Return to Training:** Softer surfaces (trails); avoid downhill and cambered surfaces; cross-train in pool/bike

---

### Plantar Heel Pain (Plantar Fasciitis)
**Location:** Underside of heel; most painful in the morning and after inactivity; may ease during run  
**Common Causes:** Rapid training increase; footwear changes; individual biomechanical factors  
**Key Clinical Note:** Pain can take up to 24 hours after overloading — not always immediate  
**Treatment:**
- Supportive footwear or orthotics to redistribute heel pressure
- Calf stretches + plantar fascia and intrinsic foot muscle strengthening: calf stretch p.82, foot doming p.100, resisted toe p.104, heel drop p.108

---

### Iliotibial Band (ITB) Pain
**Location:** Lateral knee  
**Treatment Exercises:** TFL ball release p.92, piriformis ball release p.94, hip exercises pp.118–131

---

### Gluteal Tendinopathy
**Location:** Lateral hip  
**Treatment Exercises:** Hip hike p.118, step down p.120, hip extension p.130, single-leg ball squat p.136

---

### Deep Gluteal Syndrome
**Location:** Deep buttock/posterior hip  
**Treatment Exercises:** Piriformis ball release p.94, modified pigeon p.90

---

### Stress Fracture
**Red flag:** Localised bone pain worsening with impact; requires imaging to confirm  
**Management:** Cease running; seek medical assessment; recovery per tissue healing timeline

## Injury Assessment Framework

### When to Stop Training
Stop if pain is **>3/10** during or after a run, or if running gait changes due to pain.

**POLICE Acronym (Self-help):**
- **P**rotection – tape, brace, or orthotics
- **O**ptimal Loading – keep moving; don't over-rest or overload
- **L**ice – apply ice for pain relief
- **I**ce (see above)
- **C**ompression – compression bandages/socks to reduce swelling
- **E**levation – elevate injured site

### Tissue Healing Timeline (approximate)
| Tissue | Timeframe |
|---|---|
| Muscle strain Grade 1 | 0–2 weeks |
| Muscle strain Grade 2 | 4 days–6 weeks |
| Muscle strain Grade 3 | 3–7 weeks |
| Ligament sprain Grade 1 | 0–3 days to 6 weeks |
| Ligament sprain Grade 2 | 3 weeks–1 year |
| Ligament sprain Grade 3 | 5 weeks–1 year |
| Tendinitis | 5–6 weeks |
| Tendinosis | 5–6 months |
| Bone stress fracture | 5+ weeks |
| Ligament graft | 2+ months |
| Articular cartilage repair | 2+ months |

## Running Form Cues

**Optimal form (from book):**
- Slight forward lean of upper body
- Forward gaze
- Elbows bent at 90°; arms drive in opposition to legs
- Hip fully extends at toe-off
- Foot lands close to COM (avoid overstriding)
- Cadence ≥170 steps/min (lower cadence associated with MTSS risk)

**Gait variables to assess:**
- Footstrike pattern (rearfoot / midfoot / forefoot)
- Step width (narrow = MTSS risk)
- Cadence
- Hip adduction (increased = patellofemoral pain risk)
- Pronation / foot abduction (MTSS risk)

## Strength Exercise Selection by Body Region

| Region | Key Exercises | Common Injuries Addressed |
|---|---|---|
| Foot & ankle | Foot doming, resisted toe, heel drop, ankle turn out/in | Plantar heel pain, Achilles tendinopathy, MTSS |
| Hip & knee | Hip hike, step down/up, standing hip rotation, hip extension, traditional deadlift, single-leg ball squat, hamstring ball roll-in, lunge | Patellofemoral pain, ITB pain, gluteal tendinopathy |
| Core | Front plank with rotation, side plank with rotation, box jump, single-leg hop | General running stability |

**Heavy resistance training principle:** ≥2× per week, ≥6 weeks; focus on heavy load (not high-rep/low-load)

## Training Principles

**Training Load Management:**
- Tissue adapts to load — but only at the rate applied; too fast = injury
- Monitor: volume, frequency, and intensity as separate variables
- Sudden changes in surface, footwear, or intensity are common injury triggers

**Types of Training:**
- Easy continuous running – aerobic base
- Fast continuous running – lactate threshold
- Interval training – VO₂max / speed
- Hill training – strength and power
- Cross training – maintain fitness while reducing impact

**Pre-run warm-up:** Dynamic stretches (forward/side leg swings, calf stretch) + drills (Running As, Bs, Cs, strides, bounding, carioca)

**Recovery stretches:** Modified pigeon, TFL ball release, piriformis ball release
